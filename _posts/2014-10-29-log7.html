The Google Ngram Viewer allows users to search large language specific corpora for ngrams (words or phrases) and then graphs the result of each search token as a relative frequency per year within a given time period.

The Viewer can display ngram trends that may be useful for researchers to make abstract connections for further study. The Viewer is unable to find the ngrams, such as "C++", containing special search operator characters because there is no way to escape the literal characters. Searches that included words with more than one meaning inflate or skew the results. For example, the word Twitter was charted throughout the timeline and spiked just after year 2000.

I performed a case insensitive query for the ngrams, cold war and communism plus terrorism and globalization [1], to see if these terms were collocated and whether there was a cultural shift from writing about the former pair to the latter. I also chose these ngrams because the Cold War and terrorism represent two major threats to World peace in recent history and communism and globalization are possibly related respectively. I confined the search results to the years 1945 to 2008 since the Cold War was post World War II.

The ngram graph demonstrates a cultural shift from writing about the Cold War and communism to terrorism and globalization. The year in which terrorism occurs more frequently than cold war coincides with the September 11th attacks in 2001. Beyond that intersection, terrorism spikes significantly and cold war declines in usage slowly.

The next step would be to research why terrorism and globalization are collocated, or even if there is a direct correlation as likely with cold war and communism.
